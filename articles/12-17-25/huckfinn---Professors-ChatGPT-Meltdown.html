Nation's Professors Achieve New Shades Of Red Previously Unknown To Science As Students Continue Using ChatGPT While Maintaining Eye Contact

Pantone forced to create 47 new color swatches after English department faculty meetings hit temperatures previously only recorded in blast furnaces; one professor reportedly turned "a shade of magenta that shouldn't exist in nature"

By Dr. Harrison Wells * December 18, 2025 * 14 min read

Cambridge, MA — The nation's professors have collectively achieved new shades of red previously unknown to science, medical experts confirmed Tuesday, as students across the country continue to use ChatGPT to write their essays while maintaining unwavering eye contact with their instructors.

"We've had to create entirely new categories of facial coloration," explained Dr. Patricia Chen, a dermatologist at Johns Hopkins who has been tracking what she calls "the academic crimson crisis." "We're seeing shades that exist somewhere between 'apoplectic rage' and 'supernova.' One professor at Yale turned a color I can only describe as 'if a tomato could scream.'"

The phenomenon, which researchers have dubbed "Pedagogical Fury Syndrome," has affected approximately 94% of professors nationwide, with the English and Philosophy departments reporting the highest concentrations of unprecedented redness. Faculty meetings have become, according to one witness, "like looking directly into a furnace, but angrier."

Dr. Margaret Thornton, a professor of English Literature at Harvard who has reportedly achieved "a shade of burgundy that made the fire department concerned," described her experience during a recent midterm essay collection.

"She looked right at me. Right at me. Her fingers were moving across the keyboard, and I could see the ChatGPT interface reflected in her glasses. She typed 'make it sound more human' without breaking eye contact. I felt my blood pressure medication fail in real time."

— Dr. Margaret Thornton, moments before being escorted to campus health services

The crisis has prompted Pantone, the global color authority, to release an emergency supplementary guide featuring 47 new shades specifically calibrated to academic distress. These include "Tenure-Track Fury #FF0000," "Adjunct Agony #CC0033," and the particularly vivid "Emeritus Eruption #8B0000."

'They Don't Even Minimize The Window Anymore'

What has surprised researchers most is not the use of AI tools itself, but the brazenness with which students now employ them. Gone are the days of covert prompt engineering under desks or in bathroom stalls.

"In my day, if you were going to cheat, you at least had the decency to look guilty about it," said Dr. Robert Hendricks, 67, a professor of Philosophy at Princeton whose face has been described as "a permanent sunset." "Now they just... they just do it. Right there. In front of God and everyone. One student asked me to wait while ChatGPT finished generating his 'personal reflection on Kierkegaard.'"

Campus health centers have reported a 340% increase in blood pressure-related incidents, with one nurse describing the faculty waiting room as "looking like a convention of angry tomatoes." Several universities have installed additional defibrillators in lecture halls as a precautionary measure.

The phenomenon has also created unexpected economic opportunities. A startup called "ProfessorWatch" has begun selling real-time facial heat mapping to students, allowing them to gauge exactly how much AI-generated content their instructor can handle before requiring medical intervention.

"It's like a game of chicken," explained sophomore Marcus Webb, 19, who was observed typing an entire research paper prompt while staring directly at his professor. "You want to see how red they can get. My record is what I call 'fire engine meets heart attack.' The professor had to excuse herself for 'personal reasons.' I got an A."

The Eye Contact Question

Psychologists have become particularly interested in what they call "the dominance display aspect" of the phenomenon. Students, rather than attempting to hide their AI use, appear to be deliberately establishing visual contact as they input prompts.

"It's a power move," explained Dr. Sarah Kim, a behavioral psychologist at Stanford. "The eye contact is saying 'I know you know, and I know you can't prove it, and I know this makes you very, very angry.' It's remarkably effective at inducing what we now call 'Academic Impotent Fury.'"

One professor, who requested anonymity because he was "too embarrassed by how red he gets," described a particularly memorable incident: "A student in my Intro to Ethics class asked ChatGPT to 'write an essay about why cheating is wrong' while literally sitting three feet from me. When I confronted him, he said, 'But I'm not cheating. The AI is doing the cheating. I'm just the prompt engineer.' I could feel my face becoming a color that I don't think exists on the visible spectrum."

Faculty Response Protocols

Universities have begun implementing various response protocols, though none have proven particularly effective. MIT's attempt to install Faraday cages around all exam halls was abandoned after students simply used the pre-generated essays they had stored locally. Yale's "Honor Pledge 2.0" requiring students to swear they didn't use AI has been met with, according to one administrator, "students having ChatGPT write their honor pledges."

Some professors have attempted to fight fire with fire. Dr. Thomas Bradley of Northwestern now uses AI to generate his lectures, his test questions, and his responses to student emails. "If they can use AI, I can use AI," he explained, his face a worrying shade of puce. "Now nobody in my class is doing any actual thinking. It's beautiful. It's horrible. I can't tell anymore. Anyway, here's my AI-generated midterm. It's graded by AI too. I don't even know what course this is."

At press time, a student was observed asking ChatGPT to generate a response to their professor's email about AI use policies, while maintaining eye contact through the professor's office window. The professor was later seen at the campus bar, where he ordered "something that matches the color of my face" and was served a glass of pure capsaicin.

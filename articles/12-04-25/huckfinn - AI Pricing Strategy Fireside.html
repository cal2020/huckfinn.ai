'What People Say And What They Do Are Different Things,' Explains Executive Who Just Made Them Very Angry

Panel of pricing experts agrees: customer outrage is "statistically insignificant" and should be tested on Reddit first

By CHESTER WORTHINGTON III | Business Correspondent
Published: Monday, June 30, 2025 | Updated: When We Feel Like It

Silicon Valley, CA ‚Äî In what industry observers are calling "the most accidentally honest discussion of pricing strategy ever recorded," a panel of technology executives gathered last week to share insights on AI product pricing, inadvertently revealing that customer complaints are essentially meaningless and that the primary innovation in modern software is finding new ways to charge for it.

"Passion for monetization. I love it. What we love to hear," declared moderator Brittany Catalyst of Momentum Ventures, opening the session with what sources confirmed was zero detectable irony. The comment set the tone for an hour-long master class in what behavioral economists are now calling "saying the quiet part at a fireside chat."

The panel, which featured executives from some of technology's most beloved billing departments, convened to address what they characterized as the "fundamental challenge" of AI products: customers were using them too much.

"We were underwater on customers," explained Chad Brickman, VP of Product at code-generation startup Velcro.ai, describing a harrowing period during which users were charged $20 per month and cost the company hundreds or thousands of dollars. "The new model just protects our downside," he added, presumably referring to shareholder returns rather than customer relationships.

When asked about customer backlash to recent price increases, Brickman offered what may become the defining statement of the modern SaaS era: "A bunch of angry people saying 'I'm gonna cancel' and something like a 3% uptick in cancellation rate. Not 3 percentage points‚Äî3 percent."

He paused, allowing the figure to sink in.

"What people say and what they do are different things."

The audience of venture capitalists and startup founders reportedly nodded with the solemn understanding of people who had just received permission to ignore all future complaints.

"I just ran one query and got a $10,000 bill at the end of the month. Like literally, that would happen." ‚Äî Tyler Momentum, VP of Platform Strategy, describing his own company's billing system

Tyler Momentum, VP of Platform Strategy at database giant DataBongo, shared what he characterized as a valuable learning experience from the company's early pricing experiments. "I just ran one query and got a $10,000 bill at the end of the month. Like literally, that would happen," he recalled with the casual detachment of a man describing weather patterns rather than his own company's billing practices.

"There was so much opacity between what they were doing on their side of the screen and what was happening on the back end," Momentum continued, nearly arriving at self-awareness before veering safely back into jargon.

The session's philosophical high point came from Jessica Synergy, VP of Pricing Strategy at marketing automation company CRMForce and former pricing lead at data warehousing giant SnowPile. "How you charge is far more important than how much you charge," Synergy declared, articulating what critics are calling "the entire game, stated plainly, in front of everyone."

Synergy also expressed skepticism about "outcome-based pricing," citing a major CRM company's model of "$2 a conversation regardless if it's a good outcome or a bad outcome." The irony of criticizing outcome-indifferent pricing while advocating for value-indifferent extraction was not addressed.

The panel also revealed innovative strategies for managing customer expectations, including what Brickman described as "actively shipping potential new price structures on X or on Reddit, live in the comments, to just see people's reaction." The technique, which marketing experts have termed "A/B testing except the subjects don't know they're subjects," has reportedly become standard practice among growth-stage startups.

"Anything you charge for becomes a friction point," noted Momentum, explaining why his company prefers to charge for things indirectly through credit systems that allow prices to change "without having to go back and recontract everybody."

"They put a character limit in the box itself." ‚Äî On how a major project management company prevents customers from using AI features they paid for

Synergy praised his former employer's approach: "SnowPile basically turned the renewal of their contract into just a budgeting event instead of a sales event... selling new use cases 24/7 every day." The model, which transforms every product update into an extraction opportunity, was described as "very powerful."

Perhaps most illuminating was the panel's discussion of methods for preventing excessive AI usage. Synergy noted approvingly that project management company Tuesday.io "put a character limit in the box itself" to restrict how much AI customers could access‚Äîa feature that exists separately from, and in addition to, whatever they're already paying.

When asked how companies should handle customers facing extreme price increases, Momentum offered the enterprise hostage negotiation playbook: "You give them credits. You get on the phone. You explain the change. You give them a runway."

"If you've done it right," he added reassuringly, "those [extreme price hikes] will be edge cases in number."

The session concluded with Catalyst thanking the panelists for their insights and reminding attendees that the next day's session would feature yet another executive discussing "customer value."

Reached for comment, customers across the technology industry reported that they were still processing their most recent bills and would be available to provide feedback as soon as they could determine what they were actually being charged for.

"No matter what, people are upset at you," Brickman had observed earlier, his tone suggesting this was simply a weather phenomenon to be noted rather than a problem to be solved.

The panel received a rating of 4.8 out of 5 stars and was described by attendees as "the most requested fireside chat of the program."
